LIME

Line-driven Iterative Mass-loss Estimator

Overview

This online tool is designed to provide a fast, efficient, user-friendly, real-time mass-loss calculator for line-driven winds from hot, massive stars with given basic stellar parameters and arbitrary chemical compositions (as long as the theory allows for it).

The line force is computed on-the-fly from excitation and ionization balance calculations using the “Munich Atomic Database” (Pauldrach et al. 2001; Puls et al. 2000) consisting of more than 4 million spectral lines. Mass-loss rates are then derived from line-driven wind theory, including effects of a finite stellar disk and gas sound speed. The line-force parameters are calculated following the formalisms by Gayley 1995; Poniatowski et al. 2022. For a given set of stellar parameters and chemical composition, we obtain predictions for mass-loss rates and the three line-force parameters \(\bar{Q}\), \(Q_0\), and \(\alpha\) at the wind critical point, and also an estimate of the terminal wind speed.

In addition to its speed and simplicity, a strength of our mass-loss calculator is that it avoids uncertainties related to applying fit formulae to underlying model grids calculated for more restricted parameter ranges. Moreover, individual chemical abundances can be trivially modified, and their effects upon predicted mass-loss rates explored. This thus also allows for potential applications toward stars that are significantly chemically modified at the surface.

Usage

To compute mass-loss rates, the user needs to input some basic stellar parameters: the luminosity of the star \(L_\star\) (in terms of solar luminosity), mass of the star \(M_\star\) (in solar mass), the effective temperature of the star \(T_{\rm eff}\) (in Kelvin), and the metallicity of the star \(Z_\star\) (for solar \(Z_\star = 1\)). Default scalings are provided from the solar abundance scale by (Asplund et al. 2009), but the user is free to change ths mass fractions as they wish. A change only in scaling metallicity scales all the metals (i.e., from Li-Zn). If you use LIME for your work, please cite Sundqvist et al. 2025 and also provide a footnote with the URL of this website.

Brief methodology

The tool uses (a modified version of) CAK theory as formulated by Gayley (1995) to calculate mass-loss rates, incorporating a high-opacity cut-off in line strength (Owocki et al. 1988) . Additionally, the finite disk angle effect (Pauldrach et al. 1986) is accounted for by following Kudritzki et al. (1989) . It also includes a finite sound speed correction based on Owocki & ud-Doula (2004) . For the explicit mass loss rate formula please check Eq (1) in Sundqvist et al. (2025) .

For a given (\(\rho,T\)) LIME computes the line-force multiplier directly based on the millions of lines in the atomic data base. This line force multiplier is then fitted using 3 parameters \(\bar{Q}, Q_0\) and \(\alpha\), where the fitting is tuned to always include the estimated critical t parameter, see Sundqvist et al. (2025) for details. This procedure is then iterated until the critical point density and mass-loss rate are converged to a certain tolerance. In Figure 2 it can be seen that such convergence is typically reached within a few iterations. Figure 1 displays examples of force multipliers and corresponding fits, including the position of critical t.

Figure 4 displays the logarithmic ratio of the mass loss rates obtained from some different alternative prescriptions (Vink et al. 2001; Krtička et al.2024; Bjorklund et al. 2022) compared to the emprically obtained mass loss rates. Across this range, LIME performs as well or even better than these alternative recipes based on more elaborate line-driven wind models. In part this might be because these recipes are actually fit-formulae (typically assuming power-law scaling of \(\dot{M}\) with stellar parameters) based on underlying grid-calculations for a restricted number of models. This introduces scatter and uncertainties when applied to actual stars (and even more when extrapolated outside the domains of the original calculations). By contrast, LIME computes everything on-the-fly for any given set of input stellar parameters,removing such intrinsic uncertainties. Additionally, our approach allows us to directly tune individual abundances, whereas in the laternative recipes only general scalings with overall metallicity Z are provided and applied.

The data is reliable only for effective temperatures (\(T_{\rm eff}\)) between 18,000 K and 70,000 K. Beyond this range, the underlying Munich Atomic Database either lacks ionization stages or has limited data.

We are actively working on expanding the Munich Atomic Database, and future updates will extend coverage to these parameter spaces.

Enter Stellar Parameters

Mass fractions ? Mass fractions represent the relative abundance of each element in a star's composition. The total of all mass fractions (including H and He) should add up to 1. While number abundances are traditionally used in stellar atmospheric studies, they are often non-intuitive and require scaling (typically by H or He). Since our tool supports arbitrary abundances, mass fractions provide a more intuitive and flexible input format.

The data should only be trusted for effective temperature (\(T_{\rm eff}\)) between 18000 to 70000 K. Beyond this limit, the underlying Munich Atomic Database either lacks ionization stages or has limited data. We are currently working on an extension to the Munich Atomic Database, and in a future update we will also extend to such parameter spaces.

You can submit a CSV (comma-separated values) file containing the stellar inputs and abundances.

  • If the file contains more than 20 entries, only the final output CSV file will be sent; iteration details will not be included.
  • The processed results will be emailed back once completed.

User Info

Upload CSV File

Please upload a CSV file containing stellar parameters. The first row should include the column names as follows:

  • Required columns: name, luminosity \(L_\star\), effective temeparture (\(T_{\rm eff}\)), mass of the star \(M_\star\), scaling metallicity (\(Z_{\rm scale}\)), and mass fraction of helium
  • Optional columns: Individual element abundances (e.g., H, C, N, O, Fe)

Example CSV format:

            name,luminosity,teff,mstar,zscale,He,C,N,FE
            Star1,500000,40000,52,1.0,0.243,0.0002,0.0000065, 0.00000045
            Star2,200000,21000,20,0.2,0.280,0.00005,0.000000007,0.0000000013
